Greek Fisherman's Soup (Kakavia)

Yield: 6 servings
Prep Time: 1 hour
Cook Time: 2-3 hours
Total Time: 3-4 hours
This ancient broth develops layers of oceanic depth as fish bones slowly dissolve into the aromatic base of onions and tomatoes. The long simmer transforms simple Mediterranean ingredients into a soul-warming elixir that captures the essence of the Aegean Sea in every spoonful.

This ancient soup is believed to be a forerunner of bouillabaisse, born on the Mediterranean islands where Greek fishermen would make a simple stew on the deck of their boats with fish and simple seasonings. The soup simmers until the vegetables and fish bones have disintegrated, then gets strained to create a beautifully rich, amber-colored broth accompanied by lots of bread and retsina.

INGREDIENTS
Traditional Method:
  • 2¼ pounds assorted small fish, scaled and cleaned
  • 9 ounces onions, thinly sliced (approximately 2 medium onions)
  • 9 ounces tomatoes, peeled and thinly sliced (approximately 2 medium tomatoes)
  • 2-3 tablespoons finely chopped fresh parsley
  • 5 tablespoons olive oil
  • Salt and pepper
  • 1-2 lemons, cut into wedges for serving
Alternative Method (Fish Fillets & Stock):
  • 1½ pounds fish fillets
  • 4 cups fish stock
  • 9 ounces onions, thinly sliced (approximately 2 medium onions)
  • 9 ounces tomatoes, peeled and thinly sliced (approximately 2 medium tomatoes)
  • 2-3 tablespoons finely chopped fresh parsley
  • 5 tablespoons olive oil (divided)
  • Salt and pepper
  • 1-2 lemons, cut into wedges for serving
EQUIPMENT
  • Large heavy-bottomed pot or Dutch oven
  • Fine-mesh strainer
  • Large spoon for skimming
  • Sharp knife for prep work
  • Cutting board
  • Ladle for serving
PREPARATION
Traditional Method (with Whole Fish):
Make Fish Stock (20 min)
Put 2¼ pounds assorted small fish in a large pot and pour in water to cover by 2 inches. Bring to a boil, cover, and simmer for 20 minutes until fish is cooked through and bones begin to soften. Remove the fish carefully and remove any large bones, keeping the fish meat for later. Strain the stock through a fine-mesh strainer and return to the pot.
Build Soup Base (5 min)
Add the cooked fish meat back to the strained stock along with 9 ounces sliced onions, 9 ounces sliced tomatoes, 2-3 tablespoons chopped parsley, and 5 tablespoons olive oil. Season generously with salt and pepper to taste.
Long Simmer (2-3 hours)
Cover and simmer over low heat for 2-3 hours, stirring occasionally, until the soup is rich and thickened. The vegetables and remaining fish bones should almost completely disintegrate, creating a deeply flavorful, rustic broth. Taste and adjust seasoning as needed.
Alternative Method (with Fish Fillets & Stock):
Sauté Aromatics (8 min)
In a large pot, heat 2 tablespoons olive oil over medium heat. Add 9 ounces sliced onions and cook until softened and translucent, about 5 minutes. Add 9 ounces sliced tomatoes and 2-3 tablespoons chopped parsley, cooking for another 3 minutes until tomatoes begin to break down.
Add Stock and Fish (5 min)
Pour in 4 cups fish stock and bring to a gentle boil. Reduce heat to low, add 1½ pounds fish fillets cut into large chunks, and drizzle with remaining 3 tablespoons olive oil. Season with salt and pepper to taste.
Simmer to Develop Flavor (1½-2 hours)
Cover and simmer for 1½-2 hours, stirring gently occasionally to avoid breaking up the fish too much. The soup should become rich and aromatic, with the fish partially breaking down to thicken the broth naturally.
Serve (2 min)
Serve hot in warmed bowls, sprinkled with freshly ground black pepper and accompanied by lemon wedges. Traditionally served with plenty of crusty bread for dipping and, if available, a glass of retsina wine to complete the authentic Greek experience.

Private Notes

SF Fish Suggestions: For the best local kakavia, try mixing Pacific rockfish, petrale sole, and sanddabs from Swan Oyster Depot or Chinatown markets. Ask for fish bones and heads (often free) to make the richest stock. Dungeness crab shells add incredible depth. The traditional approach uses whatever's freshest, so 2-3 varieties work perfectly.
