Greek Fisherman's Soup (Kakavia)

This ancient soup is believed to be a forerunner of bouillabaisse, born on the Mediterranean islands where Greek fishermen would make a simple stew on the deck of their boats with fish and simple seasonings. The soup simmers until the vegetables and fish bones have disintegrated, then gets strained to create a beautifully rich, amber-colored broth accompanied by lots of bread and retsina.

INGREDIENTS
Yield: 6 servings
Total Time: 90 minutes
  • 2¼ lb (1 kg) assorted small fish, scaled and cleaned
  • 9 oz (250 g) onions, thinly sliced
  • 9 oz (250 g) tomatoes, peeled
  • 2–3 celery ribs, chopped
  • 2–3 tablespoons olive oil
  • 5 tablespoons olive oil
  • Salt and pepper
PREPARATION
Prepare Base (20 min)
Put fish in a large pan and pour in water to cover. Bring to a boil over, and simmer for 20 minutes. Remove the fish and reserve and fish bones. Strain the stock and return to the pan. Add the onions, tomatoes, and celery along with their cooking liquids.
Cook Vegetables (15 min)
Cover and simmer over low heat for 2–3 hours or until the soup is rich and flavorful. Sprinkle with freshly ground pepper.
Final Assembly (5 min)
Serve hot with crusty bread and a drizzle of olive oil. The traditional way is to strain the soup and serve the clear, rich broth alongside pieces of the cooked fish.

Private Notes

SF Fish Suggestions: For the best local kakavia, try mixing Pacific rockfish, petrale sole, and sanddabs from Swan Oyster Depot or Chinatown markets. Ask for fish bones and heads (often free) to make the richest stock. Dungeness crab shells add incredible depth. The traditional approach uses whatever's freshest, so 2-3 varieties work perfectly.
